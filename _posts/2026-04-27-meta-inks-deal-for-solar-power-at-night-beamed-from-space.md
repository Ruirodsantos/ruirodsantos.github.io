You know how your phone charger only works when you plug it into the wall? Regular solar panels have the same problem — they only work when the sun is shining on them. Space-based solar tries to solve that by putting panels in orbit where the sun never sets, then beaming that energy down to Earth as microwaves or lasers. Meta just signed a deal with a company called Overview Energy to explore exactly this. It's a tiny contract, more like a "let's see if this actually works" handshake than a full power purchase, but it signals that big tech companies are starting to take the idea seriously enough to put real money behind it.

Think of it like the early days of satellite TV. For years it was a sci-fi concept, then a clunky expensive experiment, then suddenly it was on every roof in the suburbs. Space solar is somewhere in the very early "clunky experiment" phase right now. The reason Meta cares is simple — their data centers run around the clock and eat enormous amounts of electricity. If you can beam reliable solar power down from orbit at any hour, that solves a massive headache for any company running always-on infrastructure. Meta isn't betting the farm here, they're just buying a front-row seat.

So what does this mean for you, sitting here with your coffee? A few things worth thinking about practically.

First, keep an eye on energy stocks and ETFs focused on space infrastructure and next-generation power. Companies working on wireless power transmission, satellite components, and microwave energy tech are going to need investors as this space grows. You don't need to buy individual risky startups — broader clean energy or space tech ETFs let you ride the wave with less risk.

Second, if you run a small business with high electricity costs, start documenting your energy usage now. Early adopters of new energy contracts — like businesses that were first to lock in wind or solar deals — often got the best rates. Being informed and ready puts you ahead of competitors who'll scramble later.

Third, consider upskilling in energy management software or sustainable operations consulting. As more businesses chase 24/7 clean power, someone needs to help them analyze options, manage contracts, and report on usage. That someone could be you, even as a side hustle.

Space solar is still years from your neighborhood, but the money moving toward it today is how tomorrow's infrastructure gets built — and knowing that early is its own kind of power.
