Most people at work have done this at least once — your company software is being slow and annoying, so instead of filing a ticket with IT, you just... find another way. Maybe you use your personal Google account to share a file, or you switch to a browser app instead of the clunky desktop version IT installed. It feels harmless, but when hundreds of employees do this every day, the company ends up with a maze of unofficial tools and workarounds that nobody actually manages. IT thinks the systems are fine because nobody complained. The systems are not fine.

This is what researchers call "shadow IT," and it's essentially the tech version of everyone quietly propping up a wobbly table leg with a folded napkin instead of telling the building manager. The research found that most tech problems employees experience never get reported — people just absorb the friction and adapt. That hidden workaround culture means companies are making budget decisions, security choices, and software investments based on a totally incomplete picture of what's actually happening on the ground.

Here's where it gets interesting for you, whether you're a regular employee or running a small business.

**Ways to turn this into money saved or earned:**

**1. If you run a small business, do a monthly "tech annoyance" check-in.** Literally ask your team one question: "What slows you down most this week?" You don't need expensive software to surface these problems — a five-minute conversation works. Each friction point you fix can save hours per employee per week, and that time compounds into real money.

**2. Freelancers and consultants, this is a sellable skill.** Many small and mid-sized businesses have no idea their internal tech is bleeding productivity. If you have any operations or project management background, offering a simple "tech audit" — interviewing staff and mapping their actual workflows versus official tools — is a legitimate service people will pay for. You're not fixing code, you're just asking the right questions.

**3. If you're an employee, document your workarounds.** Keep a simple note of every time you use an unofficial tool or skip a broken process. In your next review or one-on-one, present it as "here's where I think we're losing time." That kind of proactive thinking gets noticed and positions you as someone who thinks like a manager, not just someone who does tasks.

The hidden cost of ignored tech problems isn't a tech problem — it's a communication problem, and fixing it is often free.
