India has one of the fastest-growing smartphone populations on the planet right now. Hundreds of millions of people are coming online for the first time, downloading apps, streaming shows, and using AI tools. Think of it like a massive new shopping mall opening up — the foot traffic is incredible. The problem is that most of the shops inside are owned by the same big global companies: Google, Meta, Netflix, and a handful of AI players. Local businesses are getting some customers, but the landlord is collecting most of the rent. Spending per user in India is still much lower than in the US or Europe, but the sheer volume of users means the total pie is getting very attractive very fast.

The AI piece of this is particularly interesting. AI-powered apps — things like writing assistants, image tools, language learning apps, and customer service bots — are growing quickly among Indian users. This matters because AI makes it cheaper than ever to build something that actually works well in multiple languages and for people who are new to smartphones. A small team or even a solo developer can now build an app that would have taken a large company years to make, because AI handles a lot of the hard stuff like translation, content generation, and personalization. The window for getting in early on an underserved market is genuinely open right now.

So how do you actually make this useful for your wallet? First, if you're a freelancer or small business owner, consider building your client base or offering services specifically for Indian-market businesses looking to reach this audience — content localization, social media management, or AI-assisted customer support setup are all in demand and still undersupplied. Second, if you have any interest in app development, tools like no-code platforms combined with AI make it realistic to build a simple utility app targeting Indian users in a specific niche — local language recipe apps, small business invoicing tools, hyperlocal service finders — and monetize through ads or small subscription fees. Third, keep an eye on publicly traded companies that have strong India exposure in the streaming and fintech space, because as spending per user slowly catches up to global averages, those early-positioned platforms will see significant revenue growth without needing to acquire many new users.

The real opportunity here isn't just watching big companies win — it's understanding that a market this size, still early in its AI adoption curve, leaves plenty of room for smaller players who move with intention.
