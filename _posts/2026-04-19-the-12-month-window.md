There's a quiet joke making the rounds in Silicon Valley right now. Startup founders building AI tools will sometimes admit, with a nervous laugh, that their entire business depends on the big AI companies — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic — not noticing what they're doing for about twelve more months. Once the giants decide to add that feature natively, the startup's main selling point evaporates overnight. It's a bit like opening a great little photo printing shop right before your neighborhood got a CVS with a photo kiosk on every corner. The timing matters enormously.

What's actually happening is that the foundation model companies are still figuring out what they want to be when they grow up. Right now they build the engine, and thousands of smaller companies build the car around it. But engines and cars have a way of merging over time. The small companies filling gaps — AI tools for lawyers, AI writing assistants for real estate agents, AI customer service bots for small shops — are racing to build loyal audiences and sticky habits before the gap they're filling just becomes a standard feature. Some will get acquired. Some will get steamrolled. A few will find a niche defensible enough to survive.

For regular people and small business owners, this timeline is actually useful information. Here's how to think about it practically.

First, if there's an AI tool you've been meaning to try for your work, try it now while competition is keeping prices low and features generous. Many of these startups are in growth mode, offering free tiers or cheap subscriptions to grab users. In six to twelve months, that same functionality might be locked behind a bigger company's premium plan.

Second, if you have any skill or knowledge in a specific industry — say you're a nurse, a contractor, or a bookkeeper — consider building a small consulting practice around helping people in your field use AI tools well. You have a window where that expertise is genuinely scarce and valuable, before the tools become simple enough that everyone figures it out themselves.

Third, if you're a small business owner using one of these niche AI tools, don't build your whole operation around one that could disappear. Use them, enjoy the efficiency gains, but keep your processes documented so you can switch when needed. Treat these tools like great seasonal produce — wonderful right now, but plan for the menu to change.

The honest truth is that right now is probably the most interesting and affordable time to experiment with AI tools, precisely because the window is open but nobody knows exactly when it closes.
