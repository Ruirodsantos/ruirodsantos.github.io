There's a quiet joke circulating among AI startup founders right now. They build their product, get some traction, maybe raise some funding, and then whisper to each other: "We've got maybe a year before OpenAI or Google just... does this." It's a bit like opening a really good burger stand right next to an empty lot, knowing a McDonald's is eventually going to be built there. You're not wrong to open the stand. You just shouldn't be surprised when the golden arches show up. The big AI labs keep expanding what their core products can do, and every time they add a feature, a few startups that built exactly that feature become a lot less necessary.

What this really means is that the AI landscape is reshuffling constantly. The tools that feel cutting-edge and specialized today might just be a menu option inside ChatGPT by this time next year. This isn't doom and gloom though, it's actually useful information. It tells you something important about where the real value is settling: not in generic AI tricks that anyone can replicate, but in specific, deeply understood problems for specific kinds of people. A tool that does "AI writing" is vulnerable. A tool that knows the exact terminology, compliance rules, and workflow of, say, independent insurance adjusters? That's much harder for a giant foundation model to swallow overnight.

So what does this mean for you, practically?

If you run a small business, this is actually a good time to experiment freely with AI tools without paying too much for annual subscriptions. A lot of specialized AI tools are cheap or even free right now because startups are competing aggressively before the window closes. Use them while they're scrappy and hungry for customers. Get the value, stay flexible, don't lock in long contracts.

If you're thinking about freelancing or consulting, build your expertise around a specific industry plus AI, not just AI alone. Accountants who deeply understand AI tools for bookkeeping, or contractors who use AI for project estimates and client communication, are genuinely hard to replace. The combo is the moat.

If you have any tech-adjacent skills, the startup scramble creates real hiring opportunities right now. Customer success roles, prompt specialists, and AI trainers at smaller startups are often easier to land than big-company jobs, pay reasonably well, and teach you a ton fast.

The window cuts both ways: it's closing for some startups, but it's wide open for people who move with intention right now.
