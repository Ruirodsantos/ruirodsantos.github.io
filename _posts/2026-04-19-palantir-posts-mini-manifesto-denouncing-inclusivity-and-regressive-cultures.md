Palantir, a big data and AI company that works heavily with the US government and military, just published what amounts to a company values statement that rejects diversity and inclusion programs and positions itself as a defender of Western civilization. It's gotten a lot of attention, partly because Palantir's software is the engine behind a lot of immigration enforcement operations, and partly because this kind of public ideological positioning from a major AI company is unusual. Think of it like a contractor who builds the surveillance cameras for your neighborhood suddenly putting a very specific political sign in their front yard — it tells you something about who you're dealing with.

Why does this matter to regular people? Because Palantir isn't just some think tank with opinions. Their software actually processes and analyzes massive amounts of data to help governments and agencies make decisions — about who gets flagged at a border, which neighborhoods get policed more heavily, how military targeting works. When a company that builds those tools announces a strong ideological worldview, it's worth paying attention, because the tools they build tend to reflect the values of the people building them. Algorithms aren't neutral. They're made by humans with beliefs, and those beliefs shape what gets prioritized.

So here's how to think practically about this if you're a regular person or small business owner. First, if your company uses any AI-powered data analytics tools, now is a good time to actually read the values statements of those vendors. It sounds boring, but who builds your tools affects how they work and who they work for. Second, if you're a small business that sells to government clients, understanding which AI platforms are favored by current administrations can help you make smarter partnership and contracting decisions — right now, companies aligned with certain political positions are winning more federal contracts. Third, if you work in HR or hiring, this is a reminder to audit whatever AI screening tools you use. As some companies move away from DEI-influenced AI hiring tools, others are filling that gap, and the market for alternative hiring software is genuinely growing — there may be a business opportunity there if you have that background.

You don't have to agree or disagree with any company's politics to understand that the people who build AI tools shape what those tools do, and that's worth knowing before you hand over your data.
