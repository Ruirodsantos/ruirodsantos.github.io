OpenAI is going through something a lot of successful companies go through — that awkward phase where you've built something people love, but now you have to figure out how to actually run a real business without breaking what made it great in the first place. Think of it like a beloved local bakery that suddenly has lines around the block. Do you open more locations? Bring in investors? Franchise? Every choice has tradeoffs, and the wrong one can ruin the croissants. OpenAI is wrestling with similar questions, just with billions of dollars and AI chatbots instead of pastries.

The two core tensions are basically these: how do you make enough money to fund the insane cost of building and running AI systems, and how do you stay competitive when tech giants like Google and Microsoft have deeper pockets and armies of engineers? Their recent acquisitions — buying other companies to add new capabilities or talent — are their way of trying to solve both at once. It's like buying a rival bakery not for the building, but because they have a master pastry chef and a loyal customer base you don't have yet. Smart move if it works, expensive distraction if it doesn't.

What does any of this mean for you? Honestly, quite a bit. When big AI companies are under pressure to turn a profit, they push harder to make their tools genuinely useful so businesses will actually pay for them. That pressure is good for regular users because the products get better faster.

Here are three concrete ways to use this moment to your advantage. First, if you're a small business owner, right now is the best time to trial the paid tiers of AI tools like ChatGPT or Claude. Companies are aggressively pricing these to attract subscribers, and the features gap between free and paid has gotten significant. Second, freelancers can position themselves as AI integration helpers for local small businesses — restaurants, salons, contractors — who know they should be using this stuff but have no idea where to start. Charge a flat fee to set up their AI tools and write their initial prompts. Third, if you have any savings to put to work, watch how OpenAI's business model evolves. Companies building on top of their API, not OpenAI itself, are often where the quieter investment opportunities live.

The bottom line: OpenAI's growing pains are actually your opportunity to get in while the tools are still being practically given away.
