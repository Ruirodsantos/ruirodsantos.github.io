If you've ever tried to get multiple people at a company to follow the same process — like onboarding a new client or handling a refund — you know how quickly things fall apart when someone skips a step or hands off incomplete information. Now imagine that instead of people, you have AI agents doing those handoffs, and the underlying process was designed for humans, not software. That's exactly the mess enterprise companies are dealing with right now. Their AI is smart enough, but the plumbing underneath it was never designed to handle automated agents making dozens of decisions in sequence. Things break, tasks get dropped, and nobody can easily figure out where it went wrong.

What Salesforce is building is essentially a traffic control system for AI workflows. Think of it like replacing a chaotic four-way stop with a proper roundabout — the cars (AI agents) are the same, but now there's structure that keeps everyone moving in the right direction without collisions. Instead of an AI agent just winging it through a business process, this new layer enforces specific steps, checkpoints, and handoffs in a predictable order. It's less glamorous than talking about "intelligent AI," but honestly this kind of boring infrastructure work is what makes AI actually reliable in the real world.

So what does this mean for you, practically speaking? A few ideas:

If you're a freelancer or consultant who helps small businesses implement tools like Salesforce, HubSpot, or similar CRMs, this is a good moment to learn the basics of AI workflow design. Companies will pay real money for someone who can audit their existing processes and redesign them to work with AI agents — not just set up the software, but make sure the steps are logical and handoffs are clean.

If you run a small business using any kind of automation — even basic stuff like Zapier or Make — it's worth spending an afternoon mapping out your actual workflows on paper before you add AI to them. Broken human processes become broken AI processes, just faster and at scale. Fixing the foundation now saves expensive cleanup later.

If you're job hunting or looking to upskill, terms like "agentic workflow design" and "AI operations" are starting to appear in job listings. You don't need to be an engineer — understanding process design and where AI fits is becoming a genuinely hireable skill that doesn't require a computer science degree.

The big lesson here is that AI doesn't fix messy processes — it just runs them at a speed where the mess becomes impossible to ignore.
