So here's a fun thing that happens a lot in the AI world right now: giant companies essentially loan each other money in a big circle. Amazon gives Anthropic $5 billion. Anthropic promises to spend $100 billion running its AI systems on Amazon's cloud computers. Amazon gets that money back, plus a cut of Anthropic's business, plus Anthropic becomes deeply dependent on Amazon's infrastructure. It's a bit like a landlord giving their tenant a down payment, knowing the tenant has to pay it all back in rent, plus more. Everyone looks like they're winning, and honestly, they kind of are.

What this really means practically is that Anthropic — the company behind the Claude AI assistant — is now very firmly in Amazon's corner. When you use Claude for anything, there's a good chance it's running on Amazon's servers. These big cloud deals shape which AI tools get better faster, because more computing power means faster improvements. Think of it like a restaurant that just locked in a deal with one specific food supplier for the next decade. The menu is going to reflect that relationship whether you notice it or not.

Now, how does this actually matter to you or your small business? A few real ways:

Amazon keeps investing heavily in keeping AWS competitive, which means prices for running AI tools tend to stay reasonable or even drop over time. If you're already using any AWS-based tools — or thinking about it — now is a decent time to lock in longer-term pricing agreements before the market shifts.

Claude, Anthropic's AI, is available through Amazon Bedrock (their AI service for businesses). If you've only been using ChatGPT, it's worth testing Claude for things like customer emails, drafting contracts, or summarizing long documents. Different AI tools have different strengths, and having a free or low-cost backup option protects you if one service has an outage or raises prices.

If you do any freelance work in tech, marketing, or content, understanding how these cloud-AI relationships work makes you more valuable to clients. Small businesses are confused by all of this. Being the person who can say "here's which AI tool to use and why" is a real skill people will pay for right now.

The bottom line: the biggest money moves in AI are happening between corporations, but the ripple effects — better tools, competitive pricing, more choices — land right in your lap if you're paying attention.
