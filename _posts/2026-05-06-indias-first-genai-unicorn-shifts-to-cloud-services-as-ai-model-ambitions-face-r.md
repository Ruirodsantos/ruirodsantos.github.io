So here's what happened: India's first generative AI unicorn — a company that was valued at over a billion dollars pretty quickly — has quietly shifted direction. Instead of building its own AI models (think of that like trying to make your own smartphone operating system from scratch), it's now focusing on selling cloud computing services. It's basically the difference between making the recipe versus renting out the kitchen. This isn't a failure exactly, it's more like a reality check. Building and training large AI models costs an enormous amount of money — we're talking hundreds of millions of dollars in computing power alone — and that's a tough hill to climb when you're competing against companies like Google and OpenAI who have essentially unlimited resources.

This kind of pivot is actually more common than the headlines suggest. A lot of AI startups around the world started out wanting to build "the next GPT" and quietly discovered that selling the picks and shovels — the infrastructure other people need to build AI things — is a much more sustainable business. Cloud services are the boring but profitable backbone of the whole AI industry. It's like the Gold Rush: the people who got rich weren't always the miners, they were the ones selling jeans and shovels. Krutrim is essentially making that same bet, just a few years into their journey rather than before they started.

So what does this mean for you practically? A few things worth thinking about:

If you run a small business in India (or anywhere), watch for competitive cloud pricing from newer providers like this. When companies pivot to cloud services, they often price aggressively to grab market share. That could mean cheaper storage, cheaper computing, or cheaper tools for running your own AI workflows — all things that can genuinely cut your monthly software costs.

If you're a freelancer or consultant with any tech background, there's real money in helping small and medium businesses figure out which cloud provider actually suits their needs. Most business owners find this stuff confusing, and a straightforward advisor who can compare options and set things up is worth paying for.

If you're thinking about building any kind of AI-powered product or side project, the growing competition in cloud services means your costs to experiment are getting lower every few months. Tools that would have cost thousands to run two years ago are getting cheaper. Now is genuinely a reasonable time to start tinkering.

The real lesson here isn't about one company stumbling — it's that the messy, unglamorous infrastructure layer of AI is where a lot of the actual money gets made.
