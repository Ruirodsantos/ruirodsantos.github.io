So two of the biggest names in AI — Anthropic (the folks behind Claude) and OpenAI (the ChatGPT people) — have basically decided they want a bigger slice of the corporate world. They've each teamed up with financial partners to push their AI tools harder into large companies. Think of it like two competing coffee shop chains both suddenly deciding to franchise aggressively at the same time. They're not just selling you a cup anymore — they're trying to get their machines into every office break room in the country.

What this means practically is that these companies are moving from "cool tech demo" to "serious business infrastructure." Enterprise sales is a completely different game than getting someone to download an app. Big companies have procurement teams, legal reviews, security audits, and long contract cycles. By partnering with established financial and asset management firms, Anthropic and OpenAI are essentially borrowing credibility and sales networks that would take them years to build on their own. It's a smart shortcut — like hiring a local guide when you're navigating an unfamiliar city.

Here's where it gets interesting for you. When the big players fight over corporate clients, they tend to lower prices and improve their tools to stay competitive. That's already happening, and regular people and small business owners can ride that wave right now.

First, if you run any kind of small business, this is a good time to experiment with the business tiers of Claude or ChatGPT. As competition heats up, both are offering more features at lower price points to attract customers at every level. A $20-$30 monthly subscription can replace tasks you might otherwise pay a contractor for — drafting proposals, summarizing long documents, writing job postings.

Second, if you have any skills adjacent to AI — project management, writing, training, HR — consider positioning yourself as someone who helps small and mid-sized businesses actually implement these tools. The big enterprise deals get the headlines, but thousands of medium-sized companies are going to need help figuring out how to use AI day-to-day. That gap is a real freelance or consulting opportunity right now.

Third, keep an eye on job boards at companies that just announced AI partnerships. When organizations commit to enterprise AI, they immediately need people to manage it internally — often called AI operations or prompt engineering roles. These are real jobs being posted right now, and they don't always require a technical degree.

The takeaway: when giants compete, smart regular people find ways to benefit from the crossfire.
