Jensen Huang, the guy who essentially sells the engines that power most of the AI world, recently pushed back on the idea that AI is coming for everyone's jobs. His argument is that instead of eliminating work, AI is actually spinning up entirely new categories of jobs that didn't exist before. Think about it like the invention of the car. Yes, it put buggy whip makers out of business. But it also created mechanics, gas station attendants, traffic engineers, driving instructors, and eventually Uber drivers. The net result was more jobs, just different ones. That's essentially the bet Huang is making about AI.

Now, is he completely unbiased here? Not exactly. The man sells the chips that run AI, so he has a financial interest in people feeling good about the technology. But that doesn't automatically make him wrong either. What history does show us is that big technology shifts tend to destroy some jobs and create others, and the people who do best are the ones who move toward the new thing rather than away from it. The honest truth is nobody knows exactly how this plays out, but sitting on the sidelines waiting to find out is probably the riskiest move of all.

So here's where this gets practical for you. First, if you run a small business, start using AI tools to handle the repetitive stuff, like drafting emails, creating social media posts, or summarizing customer feedback. This frees up your time to do the things that actually require a human. That's not job elimination, that's a one-person team punching like a five-person team. Second, consider learning to be a prompt writer or AI workflow builder for local businesses. Plenty of small business owners know they should be using these tools but have no idea where to start. Charging a few hundred dollars to set up and teach them a simple AI system is a real service people will pay for right now. Third, if you're employed and worried, volunteer for any AI-related project at your company. Being the person on your team who knows how to use these tools makes you the one who shapes how they get used, which is a much better position than being surprised by them later.

The people who will benefit most from AI aren't necessarily the ones who built it, but the ones who learned to use it while everyone else was still arguing about whether to bother.
